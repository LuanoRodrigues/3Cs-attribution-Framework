Abstract

This literature review synthesizes 7 coded studies from a focused corpus of 7 cyber-attribution framework sources.

The analysis identifies four recurring dimensions: evidence bases, attribution methods, evidentiary/legal thresholds, and response-oriented policy framing.

Across the corpus, frameworks converge on multi-source inference and uncertainty-aware reasoning, but diverge in threshold-setting, operational detail, and implementation constraints.

The review contributes a structured synthesis of design choices and limitations relevant to attribution confidence and decision support in state-level cyber incidents.

1. Introduction

Cyber attribution frameworks sit at the intersection of technical forensics, legal reasoning, and policy decision-making, yet these layers are often treated separately in practice.

Using a frozen corpus of 7 framework-focused sources, this review examines how existing models define evidence, express confidence, and connect attribution judgments to response options.

Rather than cataloging studies in isolation, the review synthesizes convergences, tensions, and unresolved design tradeoffs that shape operational attribution workflows.

The result is a comparative map of framework assumptions that can support methodological selection, interpretation of confidence claims, and future model refinement.

The InCA method integrates argumentation-based reasoning, logic programming, and probabilistic modelling to perform attribution. 17 InCA is implemented as two models: a probabilistic Environmental Model (EM) capturing background evidence, and an Analytical Model (AM) for reasoning about competing hypotheses. 18 Environmental model uncertainty is represented by probabilistic formulas of the form f : p ± ε, giving a probability interval for f. 19 All-source attribution fuses multiple intelligence types to form a holistic judgment using historical TTPs, relevant to RQ0 and RQ1. 20

7. Conclusion

The corpus indicates broad agreement that cyber attribution cannot rely on a single evidence channel and instead requires layered reasoning across technical, contextual, and legal signals.

Frameworks are strongest when they articulate explicit evidentiary thresholds and uncertainty handling, but many remain under-specified on calibration and implementation governance.

For applied use, attribution models should be treated as decision-support systems with transparent assumptions, not deterministic truth engines.

Future work should prioritize benchmarkable validation protocols and clearer mappings from confidence levels to proportionate response pathways.

8. Limitations

This review is limited by corpus size (7 sources), which constrains generalizability and increases sensitivity to influential individual texts.

Evidence extraction was based on existing coded outputs, so any upstream coding omissions or interpretation bias may propagate into synthesis.

Several sources provide strong conceptual framing but uneven implementation detail, limiting direct comparability of operational performance claims.

The review therefore supports structured interpretation and design guidance, but not definitive causal claims about framework effectiveness.
